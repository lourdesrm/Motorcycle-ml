About This Project

Riding a motorcycle is about freedom, passion, and enjoying life: however, it is also about taking calculated risks using educational decision making! We want to answer the question: "how risky is riding a motorcycle?".

Our project attempts to evaluate ride risk factors associated with riding motorcycles. We want to educate motorcyclists how certain riding factors can change expected injury severity outcomes while riding.

What We Do?

We created Classification Machine Learning Models with the Random Decision Forest method to predict the injury severity of the a motorcyclists given controllable variables.

We assembled plots for the given inputs of Age, Gender, Previous Accidents, Previous Speeding Violations, Previous DWI Violations, Drug Use, Alcohol Use, Helmet Use and Desired Weather Conditions.

Our Process

With over 50 years of techincal experience, our team's process is systematic, detailed oriented, and balanced with diverse perspectives to ensure an unbiased analysis.

  1. We searched for a well versed dataset and decided to use the Fatal Accident Report System (FARS) from the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration.
  2. Looking through the data, we chose to focus on motorcyle accidents in Texas from 2014-2018. We cleaned our data using Python and Pandas, saving the new data frames out as CSV files.
  3. Reading in the output from the CSV files created above, we create a Classification Machine Learning model using SKLearn, utilizing the RandomForestClassifier to make predictions based off previous accidents.
  4. We also created seperate month and weekday models the strictly focused on either the day of the week or which month, to determine if there were higher risk factors associated the date. These are presented once the form is submitted.
  5. After utilizing our models to make educated predictions, we then created an webpage that allows the user to look through our data and test their own scenarios to determine how risky future rides will be.
  6. We then chose to plot out many of our risk factors to show the user how they contribute to our model. We used Plotly and D3 to make our plots.

Motorcycle Accident Plots

We plot many different varibales to show their impact in a motorcyclist's risk factor.

The number of accidents increased from 1998 to 2008 increased by 3.7 times. Since 2008, the number of accidents has remained relatively constant at around 800 a year.
We analyzed the past 20 years of data from motorcycle accidents in Texas. We observed that three of five scenarios, or approximately 60%, of accidents result in death. Tip: The best way to avoid a fatal injury is to avoid any accident!
Weekends seems to be the days where the accident occurrence increases since likely the number of riders also increases. Saturday and Sunday are the days of highest accident occurrence during any given week.
A common myth likens motorcycle accidents to be the most dangerous at intersections. This data validates an alternative view where 68% of accidents are not at intersections.
Another myth points out that accidents increase when weather conditions are different than a "clear day", such as rainy, foggy and cloudy days. However, the data evaluated shows that over 90% of accidents occurred under a clear sunny day! So, do not be so sure of pulling hard that throttle when the almighty sun is bright. Don't be overconfident.
Historical data supports that motorcyclists are 2.4 times more likely to be involved in an accident if they are impaired by alcohol. Two of the most important skills any motorcyclist can possess are balance and clear decision making while riding. Don't drink and ride!
There were approximately 2,400 fatalities in Texas involving motorcyclists riding without a helmet since 1998. We are curious how many of these fatalities could have had their injury severity lowered if proper protection equipment was worn. Please be smart and wear a helmet when riding!
